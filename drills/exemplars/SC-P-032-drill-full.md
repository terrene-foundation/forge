# Document Writing Choices as Margin-Note Deliberation Artifacts

## Setup

Dr. Miriam Tsai is writing a paper on **how AI governance failures in financial institutions resemble organizational routines that persist past their usefulness** — what she calls "governance fossils." The paper targets the **Journal of Management Studies (JMS)**, an interdisciplinary management journal that expects accessible prose for a mixed qualitative-quantitative audience.

Below is a 280-word draft passage from the paper's introduction. Dr. Tsai wrote it during an intense drafting session with an AI research assistant. The passage contains several non-obvious writing choices — word selection, structural decisions, rhetorical moves, and deliberate absences — but none of them are documented. A co-author reviewing the draft has already "improved" one sentence by adding a citation to the opening, which Dr. Tsai feels undermines her rhetorical strategy but cannot articulate why without reconstructing her reasoning.

---

**Draft passage (introduction, paragraphs 2-3):**

Governance fossils are organizational routines that once served a legitimate governance function but persist after the conditions that justified them have changed. The term is not metaphorical. Like biological fossils, governance fossils preserve the shape of something that was once alive — a risk control, a compliance check, a reporting cadence — while the substance has been replaced by inertia.

Consider a bank that introduced daily manual reconciliation of AI trading decisions in 2019, when its algorithmic trading system was new and untrusted. By 2025, the system has processed 2.3 million trades with a 99.97% accuracy rate. The daily reconciliation continues. The team performing it has grown from two analysts to seven. The process now takes four hours and produces a report that no one reads — but the governance committee requires it because "that's how we monitor the AI." The reconciliation is a governance fossil: it preserves the form of oversight while the substance — genuine uncertainty about the AI's reliability — dissolved years ago.

This is not a failure of governance design. It is a success of organizational inertia. The reconciliation was well-designed for its original context. The failure is in the organization's inability to recognize that the context changed — that the governance routine outlived the risk it was built to manage.

---

**The co-author's edit** (applied to the first sentence of paragraph 2):

Original: "Consider a bank that introduced daily manual reconciliation of AI trading decisions in 2019, when its algorithmic trading system was new and untrusted."

Co-author's revision: "Consider a bank that introduced daily manual reconciliation of AI trading decisions in 2019, when its algorithmic trading system was new and untrusted (cf. Berger & DeYoung, 2006; Fuster et al., 2019 for analogous monitoring adaptations in traditional banking)."

## Task

1. **Identify 5 non-obvious writing choices** in the draft passage. For each, name the specific choice (not just "word selection" — identify WHICH word or structure) and explain why it is non-obvious (i.e., a different author might reasonably have written it differently).

2. **Write a margin note for each choice**: For each of the 5 choices, document: (a) what was chosen, (b) at least 2 alternatives that were genuinely available, and (c) why this choice serves the argument better than the alternatives. Each margin note should be 3-5 sentences.

3. **Diagnose the co-author's edit**: Using the margin-note framework, explain why the co-author's addition of citations to the opening of the example undermines the rhetorical strategy. Write the margin note that, had it existed, would have prevented this edit.

4. **Test reconstructibility**: For your best margin note (the one you think most clearly preserves the author's reasoning), write it as if someone else would read it six months from now with no other context. Can the reasoning be reconstructed from the note alone?

**2. Margin notes:**

**MN-1: "The term is not metaphorical."**

> What was chosen: A six-word standalone sentence asserting literal intent. Alternatives: (A) "We use the term 'governance fossil' as more than a metaphor — the parallel to biological fossilization is structural" — more explanatory, signals academic seriousness, but dilutes the force. (B) Omit the sentence entirely and let the biological analogy in the next sentence do the work — cleaner, but readers will default to treating "fossil" as a colorful label. Why this choice: The sentence works because of its brevity and confidence. JMS readers encountering a new term expect hedging ("we propose the term..."). The flat declarative signals that the author is not tentatively offering a label but introducing a concept with defined properties. The next sentence delivers the structural parallel, but this sentence earns the reader's attention for it.

**MN-2: Extended biological analogy**

> What was chosen: Developing "fossil" into a structural parallel — shape preserved, substance replaced — with specific examples (risk control, compliance check, reporting cadence). Alternative (A): "Like fossils, these routines are remnants of an earlier organizational era" — shorter, vaguer, loses the shape/substance distinction. Alternative (B): Define "governance fossil" formally: "We define a governance fossil as an organizational routine that persists beyond the context that justified its creation" — rigorous but dry, misses the chance to make the concept viscerally understandable. Why this choice: The biological parallel is not decoration. It carries analytic content: the shape/substance distinction is the paper's central diagnostic tool. Governance fossils are dangerous precisely because the shape (the process exists, the team runs it, the report is produced) disguises the absence of substance (the risk the process was built to manage). Introducing this distinction through analogy means the reader has the diagnostic framework before encountering the formal definition in Section 2.

**MN-3: Specific numbers in the illustration**

> What was chosen: "2.3 million trades", "99.97% accuracy", "two analysts to seven", "four hours." Alternative (A): "millions of trades with near-perfect accuracy" — vaguer, reads as a generalized claim. Alternative (B): Round numbers: "2 million trades, 99.9% accuracy, a team of six, three hours" — tidier but suspicious; real operational data is messy. Why this choice: JMS readers include qualitative researchers who evaluate case illustrations for plausibility, not just quantitative researchers who evaluate them for precision. Irregular numbers (2.3 million, 99.97%, seven analysts) signal that the illustration is drawn from realistic operational detail, even though it is a constructed example. Round numbers would invite the question "is this a real case or a thought experiment?" — and a thought experiment carries less rhetorical weight in JMS.

**MN-4: Direct quotation — "that's how we monitor the AI"**

> What was chosen: Colloquial direct speech attributed to an unnamed governance committee. Alternative (A): "The governance committee continues to require the report, citing its role in AI monitoring" — formal paraphrase; consistent register but loses the affective impact. Alternative (B): "The governance committee's rationale — institutional precedent — is circular" — analytical summary; names the logical structure but skips the evidence. Why this choice: The quotation creates a register break that is the point. The paper's analytical frame is JMS-register: controlled, precise, theoretical. The committee's voice — "that's how we monitor the AI" — is institutional-register: vague, habitual, unreflective. The contrast between the two registers IS the argument in miniature. Paraphrasing the committee's reasoning in the paper's own voice would domesticate it, making it sound more rational than it is.

**MN-5: Negation-then-reframe**

> What was chosen: "This is not a failure of governance design. It is a success of organizational inertia." Two sentences, the first negating the expected interpretation. Alternative (A): "Organizational inertia, not poor governance design, explains the persistence" — single sentence, same information, less impact. Alternative (B): "The reconciliation process was well-designed; the organization simply failed to sunset it" — more precise but frames the organization as the agent and misses the theoretical claim (inertia as a force, not negligence as a choice). Why this choice: The two-sentence structure forces a cognitive pivot. The reader arrives at this point expecting a critique of the governance design (the daily reconciliation is wasteful, so the design must be bad). The first sentence blocks that reading. The second sentence installs a different frame: inertia is not a bug in the design but a feature of the organization. This pivot is the paper's theoretical contribution — governance fossils are not design failures but adaptation failures — and the negation-reframe lets the reader experience the reinterpretation rather than simply being told it.

**3. Diagnosing the co-author's edit:**

The co-author added "(cf. Berger & DeYoung, 2006; Fuster et al., 2019 for analogous monitoring adaptations in traditional banking)" to the opening of the concrete example. This undermines the rhetorical strategy in two ways:

First, the example paragraph operates as a **narrative entry point** — "Consider a bank..." invites the reader into a specific scenario. The citations interrupt the narrative by asking the reader to context-switch from the scenario to the literature. The reader is now thinking about Berger & DeYoung's argument instead of the seven analysts producing a report no one reads.

Second, the citations reframe the example as an instance of a known phenomenon ("analogous monitoring adaptations"), which pre-empts the theoretical move the next paragraph makes. The reader is told in paragraph 2 that this is a familiar pattern, but paragraph 3 then tries to reframe it as something unfamiliar ("This is not a failure of governance design. It is a success of organizational inertia."). The reframe loses its force because the citations have already classified the example.

**The margin note that would have prevented the edit:**

> "Consider a bank..." opens without citations deliberately. This paragraph is a narrative illustration, not a literature-grounded claim. Its rhetorical function is to let the reader experience the governance fossil before encountering the theoretical interpretation in paragraph 3. Citations here would (a) interrupt the narrative, (b) classify the example under existing literature before the paper's own framework is introduced, and (c) signal that the phenomenon is already explained, weakening the motivation for a new concept. Literature engagement belongs in Section 2 (literature review), not in the illustrative example. If a reviewer requests citations here, move the parenthetical to the end of paragraph 3, after the reframe has landed.

**4. Reconstructibility test:**

The margin note for MN-5 (negation-then-reframe) is the strongest candidate. Testing: a reader encountering this note six months from now, without access to Dr. Tsai, would learn: (a) the two-sentence structure was deliberate, not accidental; (b) two specific alternatives were considered; (c) the first alternative compresses the same information but loses impact; (d) the second alternative reframes the organization as negligent rather than inertia as structural; (e) the negation-reframe mirrors the paper's theoretical contribution (adaptation failure, not design failure). A reader could reconstruct the authorial intent — and, critically, would NOT "improve" the passage by combining the two sentences into one, because the note explains why separation matters.

## Extensions

- **Variant A (parameter change):** The target venue changes from JMS to **Econometrica** (formal, theorem-proof style, no narrative illustrations, definitions-lemma-proposition structure). Which of the five writing choices would need to be abandoned entirely? Write margin notes for the Econometrica versions explaining why the JMS choices do not survive.
- **Variant B (cross-domain application):** A software architect is documenting an Architecture Decision Record (ADR) for a system design choice. The ADR format requires "context, decision, consequences" — but the non-obvious choices (why this API design over alternatives, why this naming convention) are lost in the formal structure. Apply margin-note-as-deliberation to the ADR: what choices need margin notes, and how does the margin note prevent a future maintainer from "refactoring" a deliberate design decision?
