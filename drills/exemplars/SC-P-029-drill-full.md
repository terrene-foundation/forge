# Post-Publication Gap Check for Recent Literature

## Setup

The practitioner has a near-final draft of a paper titled _"Organizational Immune Systems: How Firms Develop Resistance to AI-Driven Change."_ The paper was submitted to _Strategic Management Journal_ (SMJ) three months ago and received a revise-and-resubmit. The initial literature search was conducted 7 months ago. The revision is due in 6 weeks.

The paper makes three specific claims that depend on the state of the literature:

**Claim 1 (Novelty):** "We are the first to formalize the analogy between biological immune systems and organizational resistance to technological change, deriving three mechanisms: recognition (identifying AI initiatives as 'foreign'), response (mobilizing organizational antibodies — budget committees, compliance reviews, pilot requirements), and memory (encoding resistance patterns into organizational routines that persist after the original threat is resolved)."

**Claim 2 (Empirical gap):** "While prior work has documented resistance to technological change (Edmondson et al., 2001; Orlikowski, 2000), no study has examined resistance specifically to AI-driven change, which differs because AI initiatives are ongoing and adaptive rather than one-time implementations."

**Claim 3 (Mechanism):** "The 'memory' mechanism — where resistance routines persist after the AI initiative that triggered them has been abandoned — explains why organizations that reject AI once find it progressively harder to adopt AI later. We call this 'immunity lock-in' and show it is distinct from the status quo bias documented in behavioral economics."

The practitioner's current literature base includes 23 papers, all published before 2024. The initial search was conducted in September 2025. It is now April 2026.

**Current tier-ranked literature base (excerpt, 8 of 23 papers shown):**

| #   | Citation                                                                                                        | Tier   | Role in Argument                                                    |
| --- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | Edmondson, Bohmer & Pisano (2001), "Disrupted Routines: Team Learning and New Technology Implementation," _ASQ_ | TIER 1 | Canonical reference for technology resistance as routine disruption |
| 2   | Orlikowski (2000), "Using Technology and Constituting Structures," _Organization Science_                       | TIER 1 | Structuration theory applied to technology adoption                 |
| 3   | Hannan & Freeman (1984), "Structural Inertia and Organizational Change," _ASR_                                  | TIER 1 | Foundational theory of organizational inertia                       |
| 4   | Rerup & Feldman (2011), "Routines as a Source of Change in Organizational Schemata," _AMJ_                      | TIER 2 | How routines encode and reproduce organizational patterns           |
| 5   | Besson & Rowe (2012), "Strategizing Information Systems-Enabled Organizational Transformation," _JSIS_          | TIER 2 | IS-specific resistance framework                                    |
| 6   | Joshi (1991), "A Model of Users' Perspective on Change," _MIS Quarterly_                                        | TIER 2 | Equity-based model of technology resistance                         |
| 7   | Lapointe & Rivard (2005), "A Multilevel Model of Resistance to IT Implementation," _MIS Quarterly_              | TIER 2 | Multilevel (individual, group, organization) resistance dynamics    |

## Task

1. **Define search categories.** For each of the three claims, define 3-4 narrow search categories that target papers published between April 2023 and April 2026 (the last 3 years). The categories must be narrower than the initial search — they target the specific claims, not the general topic. For each category, write one sentence explaining what finding would threaten the claim.

2. **Simulate the gap check.** For each search category, identify 1-3 plausible fictional papers that a systematic search would surface. For each paper, record: citation (authors, year, title, venue), one-sentence summary, and how it relates to the specific claim.

3. **Classify each paper.** Assign one of four categories:
   - **MUST CITE** — empirical evidence or framework precedent that a reviewer will expect. Omitting it is a reviewable gap.
   - **SHOULD CITE** — strengthens the argument or addresses a likely reviewer concern.
   - **MAY CITE** — relevant but not essential. Include if space permits.
   - **DO NOT CITE** — would reposition the paper in a way that dilutes or contradicts the contribution. Explain the positioning risk.

4. **Assess claim survival.** For each of the three claims, determine: does the claim survive the gap check as stated, does it need qualification, or is it falsified by a new publication? If falsified, specify what the claim should be revised to.

5. **Update the literature base.** Identify which new papers should be added to the tier-ranked base from the setup, with tier assignments and justifications.

**Assessment:** The gap check found 6 new TIER 1 papers — all published after the initial search — that collectively falsify two of the three claims and require qualification of the third. This is not a failure of the initial search; it is the literature moving under the argument during the 7-month gap. The revision cannot proceed without integrating these papers, and the contribution statement must be substantially rewritten.

## Extensions

- **Variant A (parameter change — narrow the gap check to one claim):** Instead of checking all three claims, run a deep gap check on Claim 1 only ("first to formalize"). Search 6-8 databases (Google Scholar, SSRN, Web of Science, Scopus, JSTOR, arXiv, working paper repositories, conference proceedings). The deep check tests whether the practitioner can distinguish between a paper that uses the immune system metaphor casually (e.g., in a literature review sentence) and one that formalizes it as a theoretical framework. The former is not a threat to the novelty claim; the latter is. The skill being drilled is discrimination between citation-relevant and citation-irrelevant search hits.
- **Variant B (cross-domain — gap check for a technology patent application):** Apply the post-publication gap check to a patent's claims of novelty. The practitioner receives a patent application with 3 claims and must search for prior art published in the last 3 years. The classification shifts: MUST CITE becomes "anticipatory reference that invalidates the claim," SHOULD CITE becomes "non-anticipatory but narrowing," and DO NOT CITE becomes "could be used by an examiner to reject but is distinguishable if properly argued." The drill tests whether the practitioner can transfer the gap-check skill from academic novelty claims to legal novelty claims, where the consequences of missing a reference are more severe (patent rejection vs. reviewer criticism).
