# Tier-Ranked Literature Search with Source Verification

## Setup

The practitioner is writing a research paper arguing that **organizational decision-making degrades when human managers over-delegate judgment to AI systems, even when AI performance on individual tasks is superior**. The paper targets submission to a top-tier organizational science journal (e.g., Administrative Science Quarterly, Organization Science). The working title is: _"The Delegation Paradox: When Superior Task Performance Undermines Organizational Judgment."_

The practitioner has three paragraphs of argument already drafted:

> **Claim 1 (Opening):** Organizations face a paradox: AI systems that outperform humans on individual tasks may underperform human-led organizations on aggregate outcomes, because task performance and organizational judgment are governed by different logics.
>
> **Claim 2 (Mechanism):** The degradation mechanism is judgment atrophy — when managers delegate decisions to AI systems, they lose the contextual knowledge that makes organizational judgment possible. This is not a training problem; it is structural.
>
> **Claim 3 (Implication):** The optimal organizational design is not maximum AI delegation but selective delegation constrained by judgment-preservation requirements — a design principle we formalize as "delegation envelopes."

The practitioner has not yet conducted a systematic literature search. Their current citation list consists of three papers recalled from memory:

| #   | Recalled Citation                                                              | Recalled Claim                                           |
| --- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1   | Raisch & Krakowski (2021), "Artificial Intelligence and Management"            | AI creates new coordination challenges in organizations  |
| 2   | Lebovitz, Lifshitz-Assaf & Levina (2022), "To Engage or Not to Engage with AI" | Radiologists who used AI lost diagnostic skill over time |
| 3   | Glikson & Woolley (2020), "Human Trust in Artificial Intelligence"             | Trust in AI depends on task type and representation      |

The practitioner must build a proper tier-ranked literature base for this argument.

## Task

1. **Define topic areas.** Identify 5-7 topic areas relevant to the three claims above. Each topic area should be a specific intersection (e.g., "organizational design + AI delegation," not just "AI" or "organizations"). For each area, explain in one sentence why it is relevant to the argument.

2. **Simulate a systematic search.** For each topic area, identify at least 3 plausible papers that a database search would surface. For each paper, record: fictional but realistic full citation (authors, year, title, journal), a one-sentence summary of what the paper argues, and what it does NOT claim (the negative is as important as the positive).

3. **Tier-rank every paper.** Assign each paper a tier relative to THIS argument (not to the paper's general importance):
   - TIER 1: Must-cite. Reviewers at the target venue will expect this paper. Omission is a reviewable gap.
   - TIER 2: Should-cite. Strengthens the argument or addresses a likely reviewer question.
   - TIER 3: Contextual. Relevant but not essential. May cite if space permits.
     Write a one-sentence justification for each tier assignment.

4. **Identify gaps and exclusions.** Identify at least 2 intersection gaps (topic area combinations where no paper exists — potential contribution spaces). Identify at least 1 paper that should NOT be cited and explain the positioning risk (what would citing it signal about your contribution?).

5. **Verify the recalled citations.** For each of the three papers in the setup table, assess: is the recalled claim accurate, partially accurate, or likely misattributed? What verification steps would you take? Flag any that require publisher-site confirmation before use.

**4. Gaps and exclusions:**

**Intersection gaps (contribution spaces):**

- **Delegation theory + skill atrophy:** No paper in the setup connects the delegation-theory literature (Aghion-Tirole, Jensen-Meckling, Dessein) with the automation-induced skill atrophy literature. The existing delegation models treat capability as fixed. This is the primary contribution space for Claims 2 and 3.
- **Human-AI complementarity + long-term dynamics:** Bao, Li & Xu (2023) show the paradox in one-shot interactions. No paper examines how complementarity evolves over time as the human partner's judgment degrades. This is the contribution space for the atrophy mechanism.

**DO NOT CITE (positioning risk):**

- Brynjolfsson & McAfee (2014), "The Second Machine Age." Citing this would reposition the paper in the popular technology-and-work genre rather than in the organizational economics tradition. A reviewer at ASQ or OrgScience would read the Brynjolfsson citation and expect an AI-replaces-jobs argument, not an organizational design argument. The positioning risk outweighs any citation benefit.

## Extensions

- **Variant A (parameter change — different venue):** Redo the tier ranking for a different target venue: instead of Administrative Science Quarterly (organizational theory), target Management Science (quantitative methods and modeling). At least 3 papers should change tier because the venue's reviewer expectations differ. The Aghion-Tirole paper moves from TIER 1 to TIER 2 (Management Science reviewers expect formal modeling, not just theoretical lineage), while the Bao, Li & Xu paper moves from TIER 1 to TIER 1 but for a different reason (experimental evidence is more valued than theoretical precedent at ManSci).
- **Variant B (cross-domain — clinical decision-making):** Apply the same search structure to a paper arguing that AI diagnostic tools degrade physician clinical judgment. The topic areas shift (delegation theory becomes clinical autonomy; skill atrophy becomes deskilling in medicine), but the search structure and tier-ranking logic are identical. The DO NOT CITE list will be different: in clinical research, citing the "AI will replace doctors" popular press creates a different positioning risk than in organizational economics.
