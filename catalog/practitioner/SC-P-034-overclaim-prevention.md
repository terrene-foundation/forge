## The move

Before every superlative or novelty claim in research writing ("first", "only", "novel", "unique", "no prior work"), apply a three-step qualification: (1) define the scope within which the claim holds, (2) cite the evidence that the scope is empty (the gap check that found no prior work), and (3) add a temporal qualifier ("as of [date]" or "to our knowledge"). The qualified claim is stronger than the unqualified one because it is falsifiable: a reviewer can check the scope and the date.

## When it fires

Every time a superlative appears in a research draft. Superlatives are reviewer magnets — they invite the reviewer to find the counterexample. An unqualified "first" that a reviewer disproves with a single citation damages credibility for the entire paper, not just the claim.

## What it serves

COR § Layer 3 designates overclaims as a hard rule violation, enforced by hooks. COR § Three Research Failure Modes identifies "AI uses 'first', 'only', 'novel' without qualification" as a safety blindness pattern (C15). COR § Layer 1 defines overclaim detection as a capability of the claims-verifier agent. The qualification discipline is how the hard rule is satisfied in practice.

The entry also identifies the closest precedent (Puranam 2021) and differentiates by explaining what that paper does and does not develop. This is how originality is established — not by asserting "no one has done this" but by showing the closest prior work and explaining what it does not do.

## How to drill it

The practitioner reviews a research paragraph containing 2 superlatives. For each:

1. Identify the superlative ("first", "novel", "unique", "no prior work").
2. Define the scope: within what boundaries does this claim hold? (e.g., "first formal model of AI agency costs in the incomplete contracts tradition")
3. Cite the evidence: what search was conducted to verify the scope is empty? (e.g., "gap check of 14 papers across 7 categories found no prior work in this intersection")
4. Add a temporal qualifier: "as of [date]" or "to our knowledge as of [submission date]."
5. Rewrite the claim with all three qualifications.

Scoring: were all superlatives found? Is the scope specific enough to be falsifiable? Is the evidence cited (not just asserted)? Is the temporal qualifier present?

## Common failure mode

The practitioner qualifies with generic hedging ("to the best of our knowledge") without specifying the scope or the evidence. "To the best of our knowledge, this is the first paper to address AI governance" is still an overclaim — "AI governance" is too broad a scope, and "to the best of our knowledge" is not evidence of a search. The correct form: "To our knowledge, no prior paper applies Hart & Moore's incomplete contracts framework specifically to AI agent governance (see Section 2 for literature review covering 14 papers across 7 categories, current as of March 2026)."
