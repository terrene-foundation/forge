## The move

After the initial literature search is complete and the argument is drafted, run a focused gap check for recent publications (post-publication-year minus 3 to present). The gap check is narrower than the initial search: it targets the specific claims in your draft and asks "has anyone published something that supports, contradicts, or supersedes this claim since I last looked?" The gap check produces three categories: MUST CITE (empirical evidence or framework precedent that a reviewer will expect), SHOULD CITE (strengthens the argument), and DO NOT CITE (would reposition the paper in a way that dilutes the contribution).

## When it fires

Two trigger conditions:

1. The initial literature search was conducted more than 3 months ago. Academic publishing produces new relevant papers continuously; a stale literature base is a reviewable gap.
2. A specific section makes a claim that depends on the absence of prior work (novelty claim, "first to formalize," "no existing paper addresses"). The gap check verifies that the absence is real as of the submission date.

## What it serves

CO §1 (Institutional Knowledge Thesis) requires that institutional knowledge be current. In research, the literature base is institutional knowledge that decays: new papers publish, preprints appear, working papers circulate. COR § Layer 5 defines a GAP journal entry type for "literature or argument gaps requiring attention." The post-publication gap check is the mechanism that produces GAP entries — it discovers gaps between the draft and the current state of the field.

The DO NOT CITE category is as important as the MUST CITE category — it prevents positioning drift.

## How to drill it

The practitioner selects a section of their draft containing 3 specific claims. For each claim:

1. Define 3-4 search categories relevant to the claim (narrower than the initial search).
2. Search for papers published in the last 3 years that address the claim.
3. Classify each paper found as MUST CITE, SHOULD CITE, MAY CITE, or DO NOT CITE.
4. For DO NOT CITE papers, explain the positioning risk: what would citing this paper signal about your contribution?
5. Update the tier-ranked literature base from SC-P-025 with the new findings.

Scoring: number of genuine gaps found, quality of DO NOT CITE reasoning (is the positioning risk real?), and whether the gap check changed the practitioner's confidence in any claim.

## Common failure mode

The practitioner runs the gap check as "find more papers to cite" — a citation-padding exercise. The gap check is not about adding citations; it is about verifying that the field has not moved under your argument while you were writing. A gap check that finds nothing new is a valid result (the literature base is current). A gap check that adds 10 new citations is a warning sign — either the initial search was poor, or the field moved significantly and the argument may need revision, not just more citations.
