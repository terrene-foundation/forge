## The move

When making a writing choice that is non-obvious — word selection, paragraph structure, rhetorical strategy, citation placement, tone shift — document the deliberation as a margin-note artifact. The margin note records: what was chosen, what alternatives were considered, and why this choice serves the argument better than the alternatives. Margin notes are not comments on the text; they are institutional knowledge about the writing process that prevents future sessions from relitigating settled writing decisions.

## When it fires

Three trigger conditions:

1. A word or phrase is chosen for a specific rhetorical effect that a future editor (or AI) might "improve" away. Without the margin note, the choice looks arbitrary and gets normalized in revision.
2. Multiple alternatives were considered and one was chosen on grounds that are not obvious from the text itself. The deliberation should be preserved so it does not repeat.
3. A co-author (human or AI) questions a writing choice. The margin note provides the rationale without requiring the author to reconstruct their reasoning from scratch.

## What it serves

COR § Layer 5 defines the MARGIN entry type for "inline annotations explaining writing choices." COR § Layer 4 specifies that /write-para mode produces drafts "with inline margin notes explaining every choice." This is a key COR innovation: the margin note is a first-class artifact, not a comment. CO §39 (Knowledge capture) requires that institutional knowledge be captured during the session that produces it, not reconstructed later. Writing deliberation is institutional knowledge — it decays if not captured.

## Evidence walkthrough

- **0001-MARGIN-opening-question** (POSITIVE) — The entry documents the deliberation behind the CARE paper's opening sentence. Three alternatives considered: (1) "What is the human actually for?" (chosen), (2) "What is the human for, when AI can do the work?" (more specific but less punchy), (3) "The question enterprise AI governance prefers to avoid is simple: what is the human actually for?" (more academic framing, loses impact). The entry explains word-level choices: "actually" signals cutting through polite evasions; "ceremonial" means the human is still there but their contribution is performative. It explains structural choices: standalone question works because "Rawls opens A Theory of Justice with 'Justice is the first virtue of social institutions'" — the technique is precedented in philosophy papers but uncommon in governance/management papers, which is why it works. It explains a deliberate absence: no citations in the opening paragraph because "First impressions establish voice, not credentials." Each of these choices would be invisible without the margin note — a future editor might add citations to the opening paragraph, undermining the voice-first strategy.

## How to drill it

The practitioner writes a 200-word paragraph. Then:

1. Identify 3 non-obvious writing choices in the paragraph (word selection, sentence structure, absence of expected elements).
2. For each choice, write a margin note: what was chosen, what alternatives were considered (at least 2), and why this choice serves the argument.
3. Exchange margin notes with a peer. Can the peer reconstruct the author's reasoning from the margin notes alone?

Scoring: number of non-obvious choices identified (are there really only 3, or did the practitioner miss some?), quality of alternatives (are they genuine alternatives, or straw men?), and reconstructibility (can a peer understand the reasoning?).

## Common failure mode

The practitioner writes margin notes that describe what the text says rather than why it says it that way. "This paragraph introduces the governance dilemma" is a summary, not a margin note. "The governance dilemma is introduced before the theoretical framework because a reviewer must feel the problem before encountering the formalism — starting with the model would lose non-economists" is a margin note. The distinction: summaries are redundant with the text; margin notes add information that is not in the text.
