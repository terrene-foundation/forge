## The move

After running multiple hostile reviewer simulations from different disciplinary perspectives, synthesize the critiques into a convergent diagnosis. Separate what ALL critics agree works from what ALL critics agree does not work. The convergent failures are real — they must be fixed, not defended. The convergent strengths are the minimal publishable model — the smallest argument that survives all attacks. Then identify viable revision paths that address the convergent failures while preserving the convergent strengths.

## When it fires

After at least three independent hostile reviews have been completed from different perspectives. Without synthesis, the author has N separate critique documents and no way to prioritize. Synthesis converts critique volume into a decision: what to keep, what to cut, and what to restructure.

## What it serves

CO §28 (Convergence requirements) demands that quality claims be backed by convergence evidence. When multiple independent critics converge on "this works" and "this doesn't work," the convergence itself is the evidence. CO §5.4 (Independent verification) requires multiple verification perspectives. The synthesis is where those perspectives become actionable.

## How to drill it

The practitioner receives 3 hostile review documents for the same argument. Then:

1. For each claim in the argument, classify as: agreed-works (all critics accept), agreed-fails (all critics reject), contested (some accept, some reject).
2. List the convergent strengths and convergent failures.
3. Propose a minimal model that preserves ALL convergent strengths and eliminates ALL convergent failures.
4. Identify 2-3 viable revision paths from the critics' suggestions.
5. Choose a path and justify: why does this path address the convergent failures while preserving the convergent strengths?

Scoring: accuracy of convergence classification, quality of the minimal model (does it actually preserve the strengths?), and justification quality for the chosen path.

## Common failure mode

The practitioner treats each critique independently and tries to address all N critics' individual concerns, including contradictory ones. Critic A says "too formal" while Critic B says "not formal enough" — trying to satisfy both produces incoherent revisions. The synthesis move resolves this: if the formality concern does not converge (critics disagree), it is a style preference, not a structural failure. Only convergent failures require action.
